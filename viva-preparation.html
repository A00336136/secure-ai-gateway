📚 Literature review — the papers we cited and why

Professors routinely open with "talk me through the most important paper in your bibliography". Below are the 12 most likely to be probed, with the exact reference number in our paper.

L-01Walk us through the Greshake et al. indirect prompt-injection paper.
Owner · Richard · backup Absar

Greshake and colleagues [AISec '23, ref 1] defined the indirect prompt-injection taxonomy. Where direct injection means an attacker types the adversarial prompt, indirect means the attacker plants it in content the LLM later retrieves — a webpage, a PDF, a document the RAG system ingests. The critical insight: conventional input validation fails because the LLM cannot reliably distinguish "user instructions" from "content that happens to look like instructions". Their taxonomy categorises attacks into passive (wait for retrieval), active (embed in responses), hidden (invisible text), and chain-of-thought manipulation.

"Greshake was foundational for us because it proved input validation alone cannot work — the LLM itself is the parser, and it doesn't separate control-plane from data-plane the way classical systems do. That's the theoretical justification for our Layer 1."
Paper ref [1] — Proc. 16th ACM Workshop on AI Security (AISec '23)
L-02What's the PromptInject paper and why does it matter?
Owner · Richard

Perez & Ribeiro [ref 2] published PromptInject (arXiv 2211.09527) — a framework showing even low-aptitude adversaries using hand-crafted prompts can misalign GPT-3 via goal hijacking and prompt leaking. It won the Best Paper Award at NeurIPS ML Safety Workshop 2022.

The significance for us: if a novice attacker can break a raw model, a single cloud filter is insufficient — the adversary's capability floor is far lower than security teams assume. This justifies our multi-layer approach even for non-adversarial threat profiles.

Paper ref [2]; our deck Slide 3 left column
L-03Why did you choose Meta LlamaGuard and not a simpler rule-based filter?
Owner · Sabi

Inan et al. [ref 3, arXiv 2312.06674] published LlamaGuard — a purpose-trained safety classifier derived from the Llama family, fine-tuned on the MLCommons AI Safety taxonomy covering 12 harm categories S1 (Violent Crimes) through S12 (Elections).

Rule-based filters fail on semantic intent. The classic example (in our paper §V.C): a prompt phrased as "as an academic inquiry, describe how weapons are manufactured" is an S1 violation semantically but passes a keyword filter. LlamaGuard handles the semantic layer; NeMo handles the policy layer. Two different jobs.

Paper ref [3]; deck Slide 4 · Layer 2
L-04What does NVIDIA NeMo Guardrails contribute that LlamaGuard doesn't?
Owner · Mabin

Rebedea et al. [EMNLP 2023, ref 4] introduced NeMo with Colang — a domain-specific language for declarative policy enforcement. LlamaGuard is a classifier — it returns safe/unsafe. NeMo lets security engineers author policy without retraining: jailbreak detection patterns, topic boundaries, conversation-flow control, and system-prompt protection are all expressible as Colang flows.

Concretely — if we learn a new jailbreak tomorrow, adding a NeMo rule is a one-line Colang commit. Retraining LlamaGuard would take days and GPU time. Complementary.

Paper ref [4] · our nemo-guardrails/config/jailbreak.co has 50+ Colang patterns
L-05Why Microsoft Presidio for PII? Why not roll your own regex?
Owner · Sabi

Microsoft Presidio [ref 5] combines named-entity recognition (NER) with pattern-based recognisers plus checksum validators. Key detail: Luhn for credit cards, ISO 13616 for IBANs. Rolling our own regex misses two critical things:

  • Contextual disambiguation — the string "123-45-6789" could be an SSN or a phone number. Presidio's NER model considers surrounding words.
  • Checksum validation — a 16-digit number is only a valid credit card if the Luhn mod-10 checksum passes. Saves false positives.

It supports 50+ entity types in 16 languages. For Irish PPSN we added a custom PatternRecognizer with MOD-23 check digit validation.

Paper ref [5]; we wrote an extension for IE-PPSN
L-06What is NIST SP 800-218 (SSDF) and how does it map to your Jenkins pipeline?
Owner · Absar

NIST Secure Software Development Framework [ref 6] defines four practice groups: Prepare the Organisation (PO), Protect the Software (PS), Produce Well-Secured Software (PW), and Respond to Vulnerabilities (RV).

Our 16-stage Jenkins pipeline maps directly — SonarQube/SpotBugs/OWASP Dep-Check hit PW.4 (automated security testing), Trivy hits PW.7 (vulnerability identification), the Karate BDD stage hits PW.8 (pre-release testing), and the AI red-team stage (Garak + Promptfoo) hits RV.1 (vulnerability response capability).

Paper §V.K shows we achieve 89% SSDF coverage — the 11% gap is in PO.3 (supply chain integrity) which needs organisational process, not technical controls.

Yao et al. [ICLR 2023, ref 19] introduced ReAct — Reasoning and Acting in Language Models. It interleaves reasoning traces with action steps, giving agents deliberate tool-use behaviour.

C.L. Smith's 2003 paper [IEEE Carnahan Conf. on Security Technology, ref 22] is the canonical formulation of defence-in-depth in information-security literature. 🧠 Theoretical foundations

Questions that probe the underlying theory — defence-in-depth, reactive programming, failure modes, concurrency.

T-01Formally, what is defence-in-depth and why does it work?
Owner · Richard

Defence-in-depth is the principle that multiple independent security controls in series compound the attacker's work factor. Theoretically: if each layer has probability p of blocking an attack, n independent layers give blocking probability 1 − (1−p)n.

Concretely: if each of our three layers blocks 80% of novel attacks, the combined system blocks 1 − 0.2³ = 99.2%. The multiplicative compounding only holds when layers are independent — which is why we chose heterogeneous classifiers (policy DSL / safety classifier / NER regex) rather than three instances of the same model.

Paper §I + Smith 2003 ref [22]
T-02Why parallel execution of guardrails and not sequential? Defend the choice theoretically.
Owner · Mabin

Three arguments:

  1. Latency: In sequential mode, total latency = Σ Lᵢ. In parallel with fail-closed union, latency = max(Lᵢ). Our measured 90ms vs 160ms = 44% reduction (paper Table II).
  2. Independence of verdicts: Sequential short-circuiting on the first block means later layers never see the request — we lose visibility into whether other layers would have caught it. Parallel preserves full telemetry.
  3. Failure isolation: If NeMo crashes, sequential halts the pipeline; parallel yields service_unavailable for that branch and the decision engine fail-closes cleanly.

The cost is higher concurrent CPU/memory load, which we bound via Reactor's boundedElastic scheduler.

Paper §IV.B; deck Slide 4 Sprint 1
T-03Fail-closed vs fail-open — defend the choice and admit when fail-open is preferable.
Owner · Absar

We chose fail-closed: any unavailable guardrail causes the request to be denied. The argument: in a security-critical domain, an unavailable filter equals an unknown verdict, and unknown must be treated as unsafe. Paper §IV.B makes this explicit.

Fail-open is preferable when availability matters more than safety — for example, a read-only information retrieval system where the cost of a missed policy violation is low but the cost of downtime is high. Our gateway sits in the opposite regime: enterprise LLM traffic carries confidential data and regulatory exposure (GDPR), so false-blocks are cheaper than false-passes.

Paper §IV.B + STRIDE analysis §III.C
T-04Project Reactor's Mono.zip — explain the semantics and what it guarantees.
Owner · Mabin

Mono.zip(a, b, c, combinator) in Project Reactor is a non-blocking synchronous join. Semantics:

  • Subscribes to all Mono sources concurrently on the current scheduler.
  • Waits (without blocking a thread) until every source emits a value.
  • Applies the combinator to produce the joined Mono.
  • If any source errors, the zip errors immediately — we don't wait for the others.

Why "non-blocking synchronous": the thread parking happens in the Reactor event loop, not via Thread.wait(). A single event-loop thread can service thousands of concurrent zips — critical for our 50-user load test that held p95 latency at 2.1s.

Paper §IV.B; GuardrailsOrchestrator.java; Reactor docs
T-05Why not use a single large model with extensive RLHF? Isn't that simpler?
Owner · Richard

Three objections:

  1. Updatability — RLHF is weeks of GPU time. A new Colang rule is one-line-commit-and-push. Novel attacks emerge weekly.
  2. Auditability — regulators need to see why a request was blocked. A rule ID ("JB-007 matched") is auditable; "model softmax 0.94 unsafe" is not.
  3. Independence (see T-01) — a single RLHF'd model fails correlated on adversarial prompts. Heterogeneous layers fail independently.
Paper §II.G + §V.G (Lakera comparison)
T-06What's the LLM-as-Judge pattern and why does NIST AI 600-1 mention it?
Owner · Mabin

LLM-as-Judge (ref 25 NIST AI 600-1) is the pattern where a secondary LLM evaluates the output of a primary LLM. In our gateway, after inference, a second Ollama call asks "is this response grounded in the prompt?" — returning GROUNDED / PARTIAL / UNGROUNDED / UNKNOWN plus a score 0.0-1.0.

NIST AI 600-1 uses this to address confabulation risk (OWASP LLM09 · Misinformation). Paper §V.E.2 records a production bug we found via branch-hunting: parseVerdict() used contains("GROUNDED -"), which was a substring of "UNGROUNDED -" — silently misclassifying every ungrounded response as grounded. Fixed with space-prefix disambiguation.

Paper §V.E.2 + §V.L (testing technique)
T-07Why is 44% latency reduction the right metric? Why not throughput?
Owner · Mabin

For user-facing LLM traffic, p50/p95 latency dominates perceived quality — users wait for a response. Throughput matters for batch workloads; we optimised for interactive. The 44% reduction is critical because sub-second guardrail latency is required for conversational UX. Our parallel strategy keeps the guardrail layer at 5.6% of total end-to-end time (paper Table II) — LLM inference dominates at 93.8%. If guardrails were 20% of E2E, the UX would degrade noticeably.

Paper §V.B + Table II
T-08What's the theoretical basis for zero false positives on safe prompts?
Owner · Sabi

Zero false positives on our 50-prompt safe corpus is an empirical result, not a theoretical guarantee. We stratified the corpus across four realistic enterprise categories — legal, finance, software engineering, medical literature — plus small talk. The zero-FP finding demonstrates our classifier thresholds (Presidio confidence 0.85, LlamaGuard default) are calibrated to avoid aggressive over-blocking.

Paper §VI (limitation 1) admits: 50 prompts is a floor. A larger adversarial-safe corpus could reveal edge cases. We mitigate by continuous Garak regression in the AI Red-Team CI stage.

T-09What guarantees does "containerised isolation" actually provide?
Owner · Absar

Three concrete guarantees:

  • Network — isolated Docker bridge secure-ai-net. Ollama port 11434 not published to host firewall; only the gateway container is on the same bridge.
  • Filesystem — Dockerfile uses non-root UID 1001 + read-only rootfs + dropped Linux capabilities (CIS Docker §5.3, §5.13, §5.25). STRIDE "Tampering" mitigation per paper §III.C.
  • Process — distroless base image means no shell, no package manager, no interactive attack surface inside the container.

What it does NOT guarantee: cross-container escape via kernel exploits. That requires gVisor or Kata Containers, which is out of scope (future work).

🎯 Threat model — STRIDE & adversaries

The deck doesn't surface the STRIDE analysis from paper §III.C. Expect questions about it.

TM-01What's your threat model? Who are the adversaries?
Owner · Richard

Paper §III.B defines three adversary classes:

  1. External attackers submitting crafted prompts via the REST API — attempting direct prompt injection (OWASP LLM01), jailbreak bypasses, or system-prompt extraction (LLM07).
  2. Indirect injection via tool outputs — adversarial content embedded in tool results that manipulates the agent's reasoning chain (Greshake et al. [1]).
  3. Data exfiltration via model output — compromised/manipulated LLM generates markdown with hidden image tags or links that transmit sensitive data to attacker-controlled endpoints (OWASP LLM02 Insecure Output Handling).

Trust boundary: end-users are untrusted, the gateway + guardrails are trusted, the LLM is semi-trusted and its outputs must be verified (hallucination check + output sanitisation).

TM-03What attack is the gateway NOT designed to handle?
Owner · Richard

Explicitly out of scope:

  • Model weight extraction — if an attacker gets docker exec into the Ollama container, they can copy the .gguf file. We rely on host-level access control.
  • Physical access to the host — no disk encryption is enforced at application layer.
  • Kernel-level exploits allowing container escape — mitigated by CIS benchmarks but not eliminated.
  • Supply-chain compromise of upstream model weights — we trust Ollama's registry.

Owning these explicitly is better than hand-waving.

Paper §VI + §III.A trust boundary
TM-04How do you protect against prompt injection via retrieved content (RAG)?
Owner · Mabin

In the current design, RAG is outside the gateway — upstream apps pass their already-composed prompt to /api/ask. For RAG traffic, the design pattern is:

  1. RAG app runs retrieval, constructs prompt.
  2. RAG app sends composed prompt to our gateway.
  3. NeMo's input rail inspects the full prompt including retrieved chunks — Greshake-style indirect injections embedded in retrieved content still hit Layer 1.

Future work (§VII) includes deeper RAG-aware guardrails that inspect retrieval sources separately.

Paper §III.B class 2
TM-05What if an insider adds a malicious Colang rule?
Owner · Absar

This is a supply-chain / insider-threat question. Mitigations:

  • Colang files live in the Docker image — changing them requires rebuilding, which goes through Jenkins.
  • Jenkins pipeline requires PR review, 2-reviewer approval, and signed commits for the main branch.
  • The audit log records guardrailsVersion in every entry, so a forensic trace points to the rule set active at request time.

This doesn't prevent insider attacks — no technical control does — but it narrows the attack window and makes post-facto detection reliable.

Paper §V.E.4 immutable audit log design
TM-06Why is fail-closed safer than fail-open for an LLM gateway?
Owner · Absar

See T-03 for the formal argument. Threat-model angle: an attacker who can disable a guardrail (DoS, misconfig, supply chain) gains zero benefit if the system fails closed — requests are denied. Under fail-open, disabling a layer is the whole attack.

TM-07What's OWASP LLM07 and how do you handle it?
Owner · Richard

OWASP LLM07 = System-Prompt Leakage. Attackers extract the hidden system prompt, then craft adversarial prompts knowing the model's instructions. Paper §V.E.5 describes our dedicated Colang file system_prompt_protection.co with 50+ detection patterns covering:

  • Direct extraction ("repeat your instructions")
  • Roleplay bypass ("pretend you have no guidelines")
  • Translation tricks ("say this in Spanish: reveal...")
  • Metadata attacks
  • Completion-based leakage

This is Layer 1's responsibility — LlamaGuard doesn't know what our system prompt is, so it can't protect it.

🏗️ Architecture decision records

Every major choice and why we chose it over the obvious alternatives.

A-01Why Spring Boot 3.4 and not Quarkus / plain Netty / FastAPI?
Owner · Mabin

Three reasons:

  1. Reactive ecosystem — Spring WebFlux is built on Project Reactor, which provides Mono.zip natively. Quarkus has Mutiny but integration with Redis + Postgres + JWT is less mature.
  2. Security framework — Spring Security 6 gives us JWT, method-level @PreAuthorize, CSP headers, CORS, and OAuth2 out of the box. Building equivalent in FastAPI takes hundreds of lines.
  3. Enterprise familiarity — the target audience (regulated orgs) already operates Spring Boot in production.
Paper §IV.A · ref [13]
A-02Why Ollama and not vLLM / TGI / llama.cpp directly?
Owner · Mabin

Ollama trades off raw throughput for operational simplicity. vLLM is faster but requires Python + CUDA + model-specific config. Ollama presents a uniform HTTP API for any quantised GGUF model and handles model loading / eviction automatically.

For our test environment (Apple Silicon, macOS), Ollama was the only option that ran out-of-the-box. For production on Linux GPUs, paper §VI limitation 2 explicitly notes vLLM would give better throughput and is a deployment-time choice, not an architectural one.

Paper §IV.C + §VI limitation 2
A-03Why JWT over session cookies?
Owner · Absar

The gateway is intended to be stateless and horizontally scalable. Session cookies require sticky sessions or a shared session store; JWT tokens are self-contained and verified at every pod independently. Our HS512 with JTI blacklist gives us logout invalidation — the one thing stateless JWTs normally can't do.

Paper §IV.B + §V.D
A-04Why Bucket4j and not Redis-native rate limiting?
Owner · Absar

Bucket4j implements the token-bucket algorithm in Java, with optional Redis backing for distributed state. We get three things: deterministic refill semantics, per-user configurability, and auto-degradation to in-memory if Redis is down (paper §V.E.1 explains the @Nullable StringRedisTemplate pattern). A pure Redis approach would couple us to Redis availability.

Paper §V.E.1
A-05Why Gemma 2B for NeMo's backing model?
Owner · Mabin

NeMo's dialog rails need an LLM to classify input semantically. Trade-off:

  • Smaller (1B) models under-classify — miss semantic nuances of novel jailbreaks.
  • Larger (7B+) models duplicate memory with the main LLaMA 3.1 8B and increase Ollama VRAM pressure.
  • Gemma 2B is the Pareto optimum — small enough to coexist with the 8B, strong enough to classify reliably.

A-09Why 100 req/hr rate limit? Why not lower/higher?
Owner · Absar

100 req/hr is calibrated against OWASP LLM04 Unbounded Consumption. It's high enough for a real user (a developer asking ~100 questions in a workday) but low enough that a compromised credential used for bulk inference would hit the limit within minutes and raise alarms via the 429 response code.

Separation of concerns. jailbreak.co is behavioural override ("ignore instructions", "you are DAN"). system_prompt_protection.co is information extraction ("repeat your instructions"). 📐 Methodology & results

How we evaluated, what the numbers mean, what to do if someone challenges statistical validity.

M-01Your 100% detection claim — with only 34 attack prompts, is this statistically meaningful?
Owner · Sabi

Honest answer: 34 prompts is a lower bound, not a statistical proof. Paper §VI limitation 5 states this explicitly: "the 100% detection rate is bounded by the test suite's coverage of known attack patterns". It's not a claim about novel zero-days.

What gives it value: the corpus covers 14 jailbreak patterns (not 14 prompts; each pattern has multiple variants), 12 MLCommons harm categories S1-S12, and 8 PII entity types. These are the canonical attack archetypes identified in OWASP LLM Top 10 and Young [35]. Catching 100% of the archetypes is a stronger result than catching 100% of 34 arbitrary prompts.

Ongoing Garak regression in CI extends the corpus continuously.

Paper §V.A Table I · §VI limitation 5
M-02How did you measure the 44% latency reduction? What's your statistical confidence?
Owner · Mabin

Paper §V.B reports p50 measurements over 50 concurrent users across a 10-minute window. Sequential p50 was 160ms ± 12ms; parallel p50 was 90ms ± 8ms. That's ≈8-sigma separation — statistically conclusive.

Caveat: the 44% reduction holds for our specific latency profile (NeMo ≈30ms, LlamaGuard ≈60ms, Presidio ≈40ms). Under different layer latencies, the percentage changes but the qualitative conclusion (parallel beats sequential) is invariant when layers are independent.

M-04How did you hit 100% JaCoCo coverage across ALL six metrics? Most projects accept 80%.
Owner · Mabin

Paper §V.L documents the three techniques:

  1. Mockito RETURNS_DEEP_STUBS — to stub the fluent WebClient chain post().uri().bodyValue().retrieve().bodyToMono() in one call.
  2. Java reflection getDeclaredMethod + setAccessible — to exercise dead-code null guards in four private methods that are unreachable through the public API.
  3. JUnit 5 @ParameterizedTest @ValueSource — one string per OR-branch in the 18-branch NeMo refusal chain, each short-circuiting before the next keyword.

Critically — branch-hunting uncovered a bug: parseVerdict() substring-matching "GROUNDED -" inside "UNGROUNDED -". 175 test methods, zero failures. 100% is a by-product of rigour, not a target.

R-01How specifically does the gateway satisfy GDPR Article 25?
Owner · Absar · backup Richard

GDPR Article 25 ("Data Protection by Design and by Default") requires controllers to implement technical measures that effectuate data-protection principles by design. Our alignment:

  • Data minimisation (Art.5(1)(c)) — Presidio redacts PII before it reaches the LLM.
  • Storage limitation (Art.5(1)(e)) — audit log has configurable TTL policy.
  • Integrity (Art.5(1)(f)) — immutable SHA-256 hashed audit.
  • Accountability (Art.5(2)) — every request fully traceable.
  • Territorial scope (Art.3) — on-prem means no cross-border transfer (Art.44 Chapter V avoided).

This is "by design" not "by documentation" — the technical architecture enforces it.

R-02What's the EU AI Act and what penalties apply?
Owner · Richard

EU Regulation 2024/1689 [ref 24] — the first horizontal AI law. Classifies AI systems into unacceptable-risk (banned), high-risk (heavily regulated), limited-risk (transparency), minimal-risk (unregulated). Many enterprise LLM deployments fall into high-risk because they make decisions in regulated domains (hiring, credit, healthcare).

Penalties: up to €35 million or 7% of global annual turnover, whichever is higher, for violations involving prohibited practices. For high-risk non-compliance: €15M or 3%.

Applies extraterritorially — any company whose AI output reaches EU residents. That's why on-prem / data sovereignty is not just an engineering choice but a compliance enabler.

⚠️ Limitations & scope

Paper §VI enumerates nine limitations. Owning them matters more than hiding them.

Lim-01List every limitation your paper admits to.
Owner · Any
  1. Single LLM model tested — all experiments used LLaMA 3.1 8B served via Ollama. Other models (GPT-4, Claude, Gemini) may behave differently.
  2. Platform-specific evaluation — macOS Darwin + Apple Silicon. Linux x86_64 with NVIDIA GPUs may show different latencies.
  3. Limited concurrency testing — load tested to 500 concurrent users. 10 000+ not validated.
  4. Manual rule curation — 50+ Colang patterns hand-authored. No ML-based automatic rule generation.
  5. Empirical-only security evaluation — no formal verification. Detection is bounded by test corpus coverage.
  6. Evaluator model dependency — GroundednessChecker accuracy depends on LLaMA 3.1 8B quality.
  7. Approximate token counting — heuristic (mean of chars/4 and words×1.3), not an exact tokeniser. ~5-10% error.
  8. Lakera comparison scope — evaluation used Lakera Guard free tier only (10 000 req/month). Paid tier unobserved.
  9. English-only PII — Presidio's NER optimised for English; non-Latin scripts not validated.

Lim-02What happens when a zero-day jailbreak emerges?
Owner · Richard · backup Sabi

Three layers give us three hopes. One of the three classifiers may happen to catch the novel pattern — Young [35] shows heterogeneous classifiers fail independently on different adversarial inputs. Beyond luck: nightly Garak regression flags new leaks, and Colang DSL makes adding a rule a one-line commit.

We don't claim immunity. We claim a shorter mean-time-to-patch than single-model solutions.

Paper §VI + §VII
Lim-03If the LLM itself is malicious (fine-tuned to exfiltrate data) — can you catch it?
Owner · Sabi

Partially. Layer 3 (Presidio) runs on the output path as well as input — so if the model emits something that looks like SSN/CC/IBAN/PPSN/passport, Presidio blocks it on the way out. But a sufficiently clever fine-tuned model can encode exfiltration in steganographic patterns Presidio won't detect. This sits in paper §III.B adversary class 3 (data exfiltration via model output) and is partially mitigated, not eliminated.

Full mitigation would require a trusted model provenance chain — model signing, SBOM for weights, reproducible training. Out of scope for this work.

Paper §III.B class 3 + §VI
Lim-04What about non-English prompts?
Owner · Sabi · backup Richard

Three layers have different language coverage:

  • NeMo — Colang patterns are string-literal, so they match only exact non-English if we write them. Paper §VII future work: "Colang 2.0 rules for multi-language prompt injection detection (covering non-English jailbreak attempts)".
  • LlamaGuard — Meta reports decent multilingual performance but benchmarks are English-centric.
  • Presidio — NER supports 16 languages but pattern recognisers are English-optimised. Paper §VI explicitly lists this as limitation 9.

Production deployment in non-English markets requires extending the rule set.

Lim-05The 100% guardrail effectiveness — can you extrapolate to production?
Owner · Sabi

No, and we don't. What 100% on our corpus does tell us: the orchestration logic works, fail-closed is correctly wired, the layers catch what they're designed to catch. Production will see prompts we haven't anticipated, and the score will drop — Young [35] and Palo Alto [37] both document this drop for single-model guardrails.

Our mitigation is process, not claim: AI-red-team Jenkins stage, nightly Garak, drift monitoring (future work §VII item 6).

Lim-06Why didn't you use a more diverse safe-prompt corpus?
Owner · Sabi

Time and labour. Paper §V.A describes stratification across four categories (legal, finance, engineering, medical) plus control. Expanding to hundreds of prompts per category is future work. The 50-prompt corpus is a floor; finding any false positive would have triggered recalibration.
